The strike near Bushehr represents a qualitative escalation that goes beyond anything seen in the five-week war. This development interacts with the April 6 deadline in dangerous ways. For energy markets, petrochemical strikes compound the existing oil supply squeeze. But it also removes supply from a global market already strained by the Hormuz closure.

### 3. Kuwait Petroleum HQ Ablaze — Gulf War Spillover Accelerates

Bloomberg reported Saturday that Kuwait Petroleum's headquarters was set ablaze by drone strike, the latest in a series of Iranian attacks on Gulf state infrastructure. This follows Thursday's strikes on Kuwait's Mina Al Ahmadi oil refinery (fires across multiple operational units) and a water desalination plant in the UAE.

The targeting pattern reveals Iran's counter-escalation strategy: rather than directly confronting US naval forces, Tehran is imposing costs on America's Gulf allies. Kuwait, the UAE, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia all host US military installations. By striking their civilian infrastructure — oil refineries, water plants, corporate headquarters — Iran is creating political pressure on these governments to distance themselves from the US war effort.

This is a classic asymmetric warfare strategy, and it has historical precedent. During the 1980-88 Iran-Iraq war, the "Tanker War" phase saw both sides target Gulf shipping and infrastructure to pressure third parties. The current pattern is a modern iteration: drone strikes on fixed infrastructure rather than maritime targets.

The economic implications cascade: Kuwait produces roughly 2.7 million barrels per day. Damage to its refining and export infrastructure removes physical supply that cannot be replaced by OPEC+ quota adjustments. Even if Sunday's OPEC+ meeting authorizes output increases, the barrels don't reach the market if the infrastructure to process and ship them is on fire.
